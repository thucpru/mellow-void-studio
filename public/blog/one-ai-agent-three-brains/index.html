One AI Agent, Three Brains (Claude, Gemini, GPT): Picking the Right Model for the Job

A practical, honest look at building a multi-model AI agent: why no model wins every task, what routing actually means, the failure modes it buys you out of, and the engineering tax that comes with it.

Jun 16, 2025

5 min read

The trap of the one true model

It's tempting to standardize on whichever model is best *today* and build everything around it. The problem is that "best today" is a depreciating asset. The leaderboard reshuffles every few months; prices move; a new version can quietly get worse at the one thing you depended on. Couple your whole product to a single model's quirks and you've tied yourself to a moving target you don't control.

Lovdes — my AI design assistant for Figma and Photoshop — takes the other path. It runs across Claude, Gemini, and GPT, and chooses the right one for each task.


Different tasks reward different strengths

The key realization is that a design assistant isn't doing one job. It's doing several, and they reward different things:

- **Turning a vague idea into a sensible layout hierarchy** is a reasoning problem — it rewards a model that's strong at structure and intent.

- **Small, high-frequency edits** ("make this tighter," "swap these two") reward speed and low cost, because there are many of them.

- **Writing natural placeholder copy** rewards yet another strength.

No single model is reliably top of all three. Hard-coding one means quietly losing on the tasks it's weak at. Routing means each step goes to whatever handles it best.


What routing actually means

It isn't magic; it's a layer. A request is classified by task type, sent to the model best suited to it, and returned through a normalized interface so the rest of the system doesn't care who answered. Crucially, the user never sees any of it. They describe the outcome they want; the agent picks the model. No model selector, no prompt-tuning homework dumped on a designer who just wanted a layout.


The failure modes it buys you out of

Designing for multiple models isn't only about quality per task. It's insurance:

- **A provider outage** doesn't take your product down — reroute.

- **A price jump** on one provider doesn't wreck your margins — move the cost-sensitive tasks.

- **A model regression** — a new version that's worse at your task — isn't a crisis; pin the old one or route around it.

A single-model build has none of these escape hatches. When its one provider has a bad day, so do you.


The honest cost

This is not free, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. Multiple models mean more integrations, more credentials to manage, more to monitor, and prompts that behave differently across providers — so you can't always write once and run everywhere. There's a real complexity tax.

For a weekend tool, that tax isn't worth it: pick one model and move on. But for an agent meant to live for years inside someone's daily workflow, designing to swap models is the cheaper choice over the long run. (Lovdes is still pre-launch, with early access opening in 2026 — so this is the architecture I'm building on, not a victory lap.)


*Building to swap models is also how you spend less over time — no costly rewrite each time the model landscape shifts.*
